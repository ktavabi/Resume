\lettersection{About Me}
  Over the past eleven years, I worked as a research scientist at the University of Washington Institute for Learning & Brain Sciences (ILABS). In 2014 I was promoted to staff scientist. I continued to develop research projects to study how the human brain perceives speech and learns to understand language. For my work, I combine psychology and psycho-linguistics experimentation procedures with biomedical neuroimaging to engineer large sets of individual health data. These datasets are qualitative or quantitative (e.g., surveys, timeseries), and I am proficient in longitudinal and case-control paradigms. I use UNIX computing ecosystems to analyze data and develop extraction-transformation-loading and Machine Learning routines (e.g., PYTHON, R, Shell) to create 3D models of neural brain activity and yield scientific writing published in peer-reviewed journals. I am passionate about data science in healthcare because of the impacts of translational research on people's lives. Since 2008, I have directed projects to examine language impairments associated with psychiatric disorders like autism; and to develop clinical biomarkers for early disease identification. As a research scientist, I am a diligent, detail-oriented worker. As such, I am confident I can be proficient in remote work. Yet I am also a talented team player considering all research projects listed in my resume are collaborations with other investigators. Despite never holding a university faculty position, I also have experience instructing (Software Carpentry, statistical methods), supervising colleagues, and mentoring students (masters, PhD).
